What is a Budget Analyst?

{% endsubnav_item %}

From the smallest local business to the largest multinational conglomerate, organizations of all sizes rely on comprehensive budgets to make sense of their finances. Budget analysts perform the vital work of developing, monitoring, and analyzing overall budgets, offering expert advice that helps companies reach their financial goals. Since all organizations must handle their own finances, budget analysts can find employment in almost any industry.

What Does a Budget Analyst Do?

Budget analysts are responsible for knowing a company's budget inside and out. Organizations depend on these professionals for recommendations about everything from spending to resource allocation to funding cuts. While executives and government leaders typically have the final say on budget composition, budget analysts play an important part in an organization’s financial decision-making process. Other common duties include reviewing budget proposals and funding requests from different departments, keeping track of organizational spending, and estimating future budget needs and concerns. Those interested in becoming a financial business analyst must have a head for numbers and an eye for detail. Strong communication skills are also important because they can help you explain the intricacies of a budget to other team members.

Common Responsibilities

  • Budget Development One of the major responsibilities of a budget analyst is working alongside program and project managers to oversee the development of the organizational budget. This involves examining different departments’ expenses, revenue, and performance to help determine future spending needs.
  • Consolidating Interdepartmental Budgets Each department in an organization has its own budget, which must be combined into a consolidated organizational budget to create a comprehensive overview of the organization’s finances. Budget analysts are responsible for compiling the consolidated budget and fielding departmental funding requests.
  • Monitoring Organizational Spending In addition to developing the budget, it’s crucial to monitor company spending closely to ensure than an organization stays financially solvent. One of this professional's most important duties is to ensure that organizational spending remains within a fixed budget.
  • Ensuring Legal and Regulatory Compliance Along with technical knowledge of accounting and budgeting, budget analysts also need to have knowledge of financial regulations and ethics, which help to ensure that an organization’s budgeting and spending are operating legally. They are responsible for reviewing other manager’s proposals to ensure they meet all financial legal standards.
  • Communicating and Advising Executives While top executives and other leaders have the final say on the overall budget, they depend on the expertise of analysts to make many financial decisions. Budget analysts must frequently communicate their findings to executives and department directors in a way that's understandable to non-experts.

Types of Budget Analysts

Budget analysts have a set of skills that qualifies them to pursue a variety of different positions, each with its own unique challenges and responsibilities. Below you’ll find brief descriptions outlining the differences between two of the most common positions in the field: financial analysts and banking analysts.

Financial Analyst

These analysts serve in a support role for financial departments, often advising on the newest financial trends and projections to help a company make informed decisions. Their responsibilities encompass many different dimensions of finance, from accounting and economics to business projections and research-based modeling techniques. Financial analysts must consume large amounts of data, make detailed observations, and then synthesize and report their findings back to other members of the finance department.

{% endimage_content %} {% image_content image:"https://res.cloudinary.com/highereducation/image/upload/f_auto,fl_lossy,q_auto/v1512586945/resources/BA_Banking.jpg" %}

Banking Analysts

These specialized analysts are more focused on projections and research related to investment banking. They advise companies on the intricacies of different industries, demographics, and investment risks. Banking analysts are skilled in statistics, accounting, information modeling, and deep quantitative analysis. They perform many similar duties to budget analysts, but with an emphasis on maximizing return on investment for their companies.

Educational Paths to Becoming a Budget Analyst

{% endsubnav_item %}

While gaining a certain amount of experience in budgeting or finance may sometimes be enough to move into a budget analyst position, your best shot is to enroll in an accredited degree program. Earning a degree in budget analysis will provide you with the skills and background that are needed for steady employment and career growth.

What Degree is Needed to Become a Budget Analyst?

Most budget analyst positions require at least a bachelor’s degree, though more elite employers may desire a master’s degree even for their general analysts. Private sector employers desire candidates with a degree in a relevant field, such as finance, business, accounting, economics, statistics, or public administration. Government agencies and some private employers may be more lenient with degree requirements, as the Bureau of Labor Statistics reports that a degree in political science or sociology may sometimes be adequate to obtain a budget analyst position.

For higher-level positions in management, a master’s degree is usually required. Many MBA programs offer concentrations in finance or financial analysis and advanced degrees in accounting or statistics may also help you distinguish yourself from other applicants.

Internships for Budget Analysts

One of the best ways to develop your skills and increase job opportunities is through an internship. Internships are typically required in master’s programs, but many undergraduate programs offer them as well. An internship gives you the chance to gain professional knowledge in a real finance environment, working under other employees and learning from their experience. Internships typically occupy a set number of hours each week and are designed to not overlap with a student's classroom schedule. While most internships are unpaid, some of the more prestigious positions may let you take home a paycheck.

Internships are often available as part of a program's curriculum, allowing students to gain academic credit and build work experience at the same time. Even those that don’t translate to course credit offer valuable opportunities for learning and networking. If your program does not include internships as part of the curriculum, then it’s still usually possible to find your own opportunities, either with the help of the school or through your own research.

Professional Organizations

  • Association for Finance Professionals AFP is committed to the success of finance professionals and holds the largest annual finance conference, which usually draws over 6,500 finance professionals. The AFP conference brings together guest speakers and professionals from around the world and includes seminars, lectures, and substantial networking opportunities. AFP also publishes trade journals, resources books, and original research to keep members up to date on the latest developments in the finance field.
  • Institute of Management Accountants Boasting more than 85,000 members drawn from the fields of accounting and financial management, the IMA offers services for students, professionals, educators, and employers. With a deep focus on professional development, IMA’s online resources can help you evaluate your skill set and identify the areas where you need to improve. The organization offers over 500 continuing education courses that cover everything from accounting strategy to industry ethics. IMA also offers test prep for industry certification exams.
  • Associate of Government Accountants AGA is the leading professional organization for government financial professionals and offers several resources for members, including conferences, networking events, professional certifications, education, career development, and professional publications. With over 100 chapters across the country, it’s easy to find opportunities in your state. AGA also offers scholarships and other awards for finance students.
